# Guidelines for Writing Scientific Papers

### Core Philosophy: Simplicity & Storytelling
- Keep it simple: Your primary goal is to tell a coherent, compelling story.
- The "Single Thread": Identify two or three key points you want the reader to remember and ensure they form a single thread running through the entire paper.
- Minimalism: If a word, sentence, or paragraph isn't necessary for understanding the main theme, omit it.

### Paragraph & Sentence Structure
- Paragraph Discipline: Limit each paragraph to a single message.
- The Narrative Arc: Each paragraph should explore a message by asking a question, progressing to an idea, and sometimes reaching an answer.
- Short Sentences: Use concise, simply constructed, and direct sentences.
- Minimize Friction: Avoid footnotes, jargon, and excessive transition words (like "however" or "thus") that slow the reader down.

### Tone & Language
- Prioritize Clarity: It is more important to be understood than to have a grammatically perfect sentence.
- Use Personal Tone: Impersonal, passive text does not make your work more "objective"; a personal tone engages the reader better.
- Be Concrete: Use concrete examples rather than abstract descriptions (e.g., a "red balloon" instead of an "abstract sphere").

### Reference
> Van Savage and Pamela Yeh. 2019. Novelist Cormac McCarthy’s tips on how to write a great science paper. Nature 574, 7778 (September 2019), 441–442. https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-019-02918-5
